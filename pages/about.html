Faith & The Philosophy of Belief

I'm active in my church and have had the privilege of teaching classes and presenting about God to the public. But my relationship with faith goes deeper than attendance — it's philosophical.

I'm fascinated by the psychological effect that believing in something greater than yourself has on a person. The redemption story, the concept of infinite love, the idea that you are seen and known — these are profoundly powerful frameworks for living. Whether approached theologically or philosophically, the question of what we anchor ourselves to is one of the most important questions a person can sit with.

This intersects directly with my love of Camus — and I'll get to that. But faith, for me, is my chosen answer to the absurd.

Philosophy — Machiavelli, Nietzsche & Camus

Philosophy is where everything else in my life converges. I've read Machiavelli on power and realism, Nietzsche on meaning and morality — but the thinker who resonates most deeply is Albert Camus.

His essay The Myth of Sisyphus tackles the absurd — the fundamental tension between humanity's desperate search for meaning and the universe's total silence on the matter. Camus presents two responses to this tension: the physical death of the body in defiance, or the spiritual death where one surrenders to a belief in something greater. He calls the latter a "philosophical suicide."

I chose the spiritual death — deliberately. My faith is not naive. It is an eyes-open acceptance that meaning may not be provable, and a conscious choice to believe anyway. Camus would call it a leap. I call it the most honest thing I can do. And then, like Sisyphus pushing his boulder, I keep going — and I find a way to be content in the struggle.

Mythology — Norse & Greek

I got into Norse mythology because of how it depicts its gods — not as perfect divine beings but as extensions of humanity's worst values turned up to eleven. Jealousy, rage, pride, betrayal — all present, all consequential. There's an honesty to it. The gods don't pretend to be above human nature, they're consumed by it. That makes them compelling in a way sanitised mythology never is.

My favourite story however comes from Greek mythology — Persephone and Hades. And Hades is my favourite Greek god, precisely because of his subversion of character. In a pantheon full of unfaithful, self-serving gods, Hades stands out as arguably the most loyal, faithful and just of them all. He rules the underworld — the most feared realm — yet he is fair, consistent and devoted. There's something deeply compelling about a figure defined by where he is placed rather than who he actually is.

Technology & Home Labbing

I genuinely enjoy breaking things to understand them. My interest in technology goes beyond writing code — I like getting into the lower layers. Disk managers, partition tables, driver conflicts, faulty configurations — I find troubleshooting deeply satisfying. There's a logic to every problem and finding it feels like solving a puzzle.

Home labbing — setting up local servers, experimenting with network configurations, playing with operating systems — scratches the same itch. It's hands-on learning that no tutorial can replicate. You break something real, you fix something real, and you actually understand why it works afterwards.

My taste in games leans toward depth and world-building.
