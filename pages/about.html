Philosophy

Camus is where everything else in my life converges.

I've read Machiavelli on power and realism, Nietzsche on meaning and morality. But The Myth of Sisyphus hit differently. The absurd — the tension between humanity's desperate search for meaning and the universe's total silence — is the central problem of my intellectual life.

Camus presents two responses: physical death in defiance, or spiritual death through belief in something greater. He calls the latter philosophical suicide. I understand the view and yet I believe.

It is better to trust in the unseen than to give in too ones inner pain.
Camus The Absurd Nietzsche Machiavelli Existentialism

Mythology

Norse gods fascinate me because they never pretend to be above human nature — they're consumed by it.

Jealousy, rage, pride, betrayal — all present, all consequential. There's an honesty to it that sanitised mythology never achieves.

But my favourite story is Greek — Persephone and Hades. In a pantheon full of unfaithful self-serving gods, Hades is arguably the most loyal, faithful and just of them all. He rules the most feared realm yet he is fair, consistent and devoted.

Hades is defined by where he is placed — not who he actually is.
Norse Mythology Greek Mythology Hades Persephone

"Not all who dwell in darkness are dark."

— On Hades

Technology & Home Labbing

I enjoy breaking things to understand them.

Disk managers, partition tables, driver conflicts, faulty configurations — troubleshooting is deeply satisfying. There's a logic to every problem and finding it feels like solving a puzzle.

Home labbing — local servers, network configurations, experimenting with operating systems — is hands-on learning no tutorial can replicate. You break something real, fix something real, and actually understand why it works afterwards.

The best way to learn a system is to break it on purpose.
